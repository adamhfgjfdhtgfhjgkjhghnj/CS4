Legal Issues and Intellectual Property

Legal issues in computing include copyright, software ownership, and intellectual property. Intellectual property laws protect creators by stopping unauthorized copying of code, software, music, images, and other digital content.

These laws can be helpful because they reward people for their work, but they can also slow innovation if access becomes too restricted. Open source software often encourages collaboration, while strict proprietary systems may give users fewer freedoms.

Privacy and Hidden Data Collection

Privacy is a major concern in computing because many websites, apps, and devices collect data automatically without users fully noticing. This can include location, search history, clicks, device details, and browsing behavior.

The problem is that users may not understand how much data is being gathered or how it is being used. This creates concerns about tracking, profiling, and loss of control over personal information.

Social, Economic, Cultural, and Environmental Impact

Social

Computing changes how people communicate, learn, and interact with each other.

Economic

Businesses profit from digital systems and data, but privacy can be reduced.

Cultural

Technology connects cultures globally, but it can also affect local traditions.

Environmental

Computers create e-waste and increase energy use, which affects the environment.

Malware, Attacks, and Protection

Sensitive data can be affected by malware and other attacks such as phishing, spyware, ransomware, and viruses. These threats can steal passwords, lock files, spy on users, or damage computer systems.

Phishing

Fake messages or websites trick users into giving personal information.

Ransomware

Locks files and demands payment to restore access.

Spyware

Secretly watches user activity and collects data without permission.

Security Measures

Strong passwords, updates, encryption, antivirus tools, and careful browsing reduce risk.

Good security also has to be practical. A system may be strong, but if it is too expensive, too difficult, or unfair to users, people may avoid it. Security choices should balance efficiency, feasibility, and ethics.

Evaluating Software Licenses

Licenses decide how software and computational artifacts can be used, modified, or shared. Proprietary licenses usually restrict copying and editing, while open source licenses allow users to view, change, and redistribute code under certain conditions.

Choosing a license depends on the goal. Some projects need stronger control to protect the creator’s work, while others benefit from openness and collaboration. Evaluating licenses is important when using libraries and external resources in software projects.
